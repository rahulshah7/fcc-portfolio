A little about me.

I started programming in 2012 when I found MIT‚Äôs Introduction to Computer Science and Programming in Python from MIT OpenCourseWare. However, in my search for an accompanying textbook, I came across some advice from Zed Shaw in Learn Python the Hard Way:

Programming as a profession is only moderately interesting. It can be a good job, but you could make about the same money and be happier running a fast food joint. You‚Äôre much better off using code as your secret weapon in another profession.

People who can code in the world of technology companies are a dime a dozen and get no respect. People who can code in biology, medicine, government, sociology, physics, history, and mathematics are respected and can do amazing things to advance those disciplines.

Reflecting on it now, I admittedly took this out of context by not reading the main chapters and instead skipping to the advice section at the end. Nonetheless, the idea had been planted in my mind and due to laziness, I used this as an excuse to put off learning software development topics such as recursion, OOP and automated testing. Therefore, over the next 5 years my coding was primarily limited to procedural scripts which automated data extraction/transformation/analysis tasks in the fields of accounting and finance.

During these years I utilised Python, Excel VBA, AHK and C#. However, as I didn‚Äôt take a structured approach to learning these languages, I would limit myself to the bare minimum knowledge I could glean from Google in order to complete what I was working on. In addition, I also blew off ever setting up a proper development tool chain or workflow which lead to short-term ‚Äúproductivity‚Äù but long-term pain. Having since read the work of Dr. Carol Dweck, I‚Äôve come to realise that I possessed the characteristics of a fixed mindset learner, so would give up when faced with difficult to grasp; abstract topics. I‚Äôve since tried to adopt the growth mindset by forcibly maintaining my focus on finding solutions when encountering challenges which are outside my circle of competence.

It wasn‚Äôt until December 2017 and early 2018, feeling dissatisfied with my collections of hacked together scripts, that I decided to take part in CS50‚Äôs Introduction to Computer Science Watching the lectures and tackling the problem sets helped me gain an understanding of some fundamental computer science principles, and more importantly, that it‚Äôs okay to rely on the layers of abstraction which allow modern software development in higher level languages. During this course I used Scratch, C, Python, HTML, CSS, JavaScript and SQL.

In 2018, I acquired a theoretical understanding of the field of web development out of plain interest and fascination with how the internet connects billions of devices together. I also gained some hands on experience with Linux and IoT, and used the Bash and Arduino coding languages.

In late September 2018 I started completing freeCodeCamp projects and gained a good grasp of basic HTML, CSS and JavaScript in the following 3 month period. I was coding at least a couple of hours, almost every day. I also started to attend meetups, which made my interest in tech and coding more of a social activity.
